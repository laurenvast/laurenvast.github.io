Understanding Users

For my research, the user group is visitors of New York City, which includes tourists, non-local students, visiting families, etc.

Based on the user group, observations and questionnaire were decided as the two methods for this user research. One collects qualitative behavior data, the other acquires quantitative attitudinal data.

3 Observations

Participants were given two tasks to complete, and both tasks were semi-structured. The goal was to find out what users prefer on the homepage on a museum site then decide whether to visit or not.

14 Questionnaires

The survey was created in Google Forms with 11 optional questions regarding both their usage of museums’ websites and their museum attendance habits.

Key Findings

  • NYC visitors like to go to museums in pairs
  • Visitors care about the hours when going to a museum website
  • Participans decide whether to visit or not primarily based on the images
  • Users became more involved when they see discounts they can relate to, and it stimulates them to visit

Insights

  • The website should have social buttons
  • The admission information should be easy to find on the homepage
  • The images shoule be informative and visually appealing
  • Discount information should be highlighted and simple

Persona

Scenario

Ian just finish a big class project this week, and finally have time to relax. He thought about his friend Daniel from college. Both of them just moved to NYC a few month ago for grad school, but they don’t see each other very often like in college because they’re in different part of town now. Noticing that Daniel is online, so Ian messaged him on Facebook on his computer.

After a few warm-up chats, they decide to do something together this weekend. They’re trying to find a fun place to go. Ian just recalled that one of his classmates mentioned a museum something called Intrepid museum , and invited him to go together, but he was super busy working on that project, so he couldn’t make it. He mentioned the Intrepid museum to Daniel, he’s never heard of it as well. So Ian opens a tab on Chrome and searches for “Intrepid museum”. He sees that “The Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum” is in the first result, and the url seems real, so he click on the link.

Competitive analysis

We analyzed five dimensions amoung four selected museum websites which share similar features and functions with the Intrepid Museum website.

Dimensions Analyzed

  • Homepage
  • Navigation
  • Links & Labels
  • Appearance
  • Mobile-friendliness

Findings

  • Use description on pictures to inform users
  • The menu should be optimized for efficient lookups
  • Labels should be self-explainatory
  • The visual user interface shoud be consistent and welcoming
  • The homepage should depict enough information for the user to understand the museum

Tree Test

After we conducted our tree test, a clear issue with our information architecture emerged. For task 3, participants were asked where they would find information regarding scheduling a tour for a school group. The correct tab was “tours,” but the majority (seven out of ten participants) chose “group visits”. While this may have partially been an issue with the question itself, we decided that there should be a separate “Education” tab at the top level.
