Objective

Provide an interactive book solution for grade 3-5 students to use in the classroom, and deliver a consistent student experience across grade K-5.

Background

Back to 2018, we had developed an interactive book for grade K-2 students. In the interactive book, there’re challenge activities to test out their reading skills.

Given the aggressive timeline and resource limitation, it was desired that we keep the core part of the existing interactive book the same while satisfying the needs for grade 3-5 students.

Existing Amplify Reading’s interactive book for grade K-2

Learning about the kids at grade 3-5

In order to gain more empathy with kids at 3rd to 5th grade level, I, along with the design team, started reading childrens books that are written for 8 to 10 year old kids. We also played video games at the same level. Based on the grade 3-5 student profiles, the product team identified the needs to focus on.


Grade 3-5 students' primary needs for the interactive book:

  • Grade 3-5 students need to engage with more complex book and activities so that they can feel more accomplished.
  • Grade 3-5 students need to be able to read long paragraphs comfortably and work on the complex activities easily so that they can focus on comprehending.

3rd to 5th graders start reading chapter books and
playing games that take longer to complete

Challenge 1

How might we provide a comfortable reading experience for the long passages?

We were able to confirm with the content team that our selection of books for the grade 3-5 edition was indeed going to have long paragraphs. First and foremost, the new edition need to allow students be able to read long paragraphs comfortably and work on the complex activities easily so that they can focus on comprehending.

Exising grade K-2 reading experience

Two column view with big font size

Students in grade K-2 are very early readers who needs bigger text and images support on every page when reading. Also they can only read really short sentences so immersive reading wasn’t a big focus.

Simple inline activities overlap on text

The questions were simple and written in a way that students could always find the clues within the half of the visible content.

How grade 3-5 reading is different

Long paragraphs with much fewer images

We were able to confirm with the content team that our new selection of books was going to have long paragraphs are harder to conprehend. Also they no longer reqire as many image support as K-2 kids.

More complex inline activities

Some interactive activities reqire students to engage with the passage and questions back and forth.

Identify problems
  • Overly large font size and low character count per line contribute to poor readability for long passages.
  • The two column view doesn’t provide good readability for grade 3-5 books in our system.
  • Toggling questions open and close distracts students from working on complex questions

Importing long paragraph to existing K-2 design
results in poor readability

Research: optimal reading experience

Use single column view for better readbility

It was decided that we no longer need to support mobile optmization for them given the complexity of the tasks. I explored the idea of a single column view. I tested a couple variations on iPad and Chromebook with some typography gurus in the company, and we agreed on a font size considering that our users are kids who are still developing their reading skills.

The confortable width range for long paragraphs

The potential solution

I explored the idea of utilizing the empty horizontal space. To test out and to demonstrate the interactive behavior of paragraph shifting to the left, I made a quick prototype with Principle app.

Prototype that demonstrates the shift interaction

Test and iterate

How do the students respond to it?

Playtesting

Even though the design looked good to me and the team, but we all knew that we needed to put it in front of the kids, whom we were designing for. I drafted a testing plan and went through it with the playtester to make sure the test plan was clear.

The playtesting team conducted 4 separated sessions with different students in the ideal grades. After reading through the testing report and watching the recording, I was able to identify some issues.

Documents of playtesting sessions

Usability issues

Students didn't notice the activity icon in the top right corner when reading

When they got to the end of the text (often just skipping forward) there were instructions to go back and answer the questions. That's when they started looking for them.

They didn’t know that they can navigate the page when activity panel is open

The testing result also showed that they were able to navigate the page with no issue in regular reading mode with the same arrow button.

Insight

For the first issue, I thought that the cause was the position of the icon being too far away from the text. So I explored a couple ideas of making the anchor to be closer to the body text.

For the second issue, my assumption was that because the layout had changed to have a lot more things that shifted their attention, the button with low visual contrast became hard to notice.

The solution

I made the arrow to be more visible by giving more color contrast, and also made sure that the new contrast passes the accessibility retirement, along with several other elements.

Challenge 2

How might we accommodate more complex content for existing activities?

Understand activity types

We have several different types of activities in the interactive book, and they can be categrized into 2 big buckets

Inline questions (half width)

There are many of them, but with a little bit of resizing, the panel on the right side of the screen would work well for all question types in this categrary.

Post-reading questions (full width)

There are two types of activities under this bucket. The K-2 use the same design because the content structure is the same for both.

Content analysis

Getting real content

Knowing that grade 3-5 will have more answer choices for post-reading questions from my product manager, I reached out to our content team, and was able to get their working documents that had everything I needed.

Sample of working documents content team provided

Visualizing the content

However, the documents were structured in a way that was really hard for me to analyze what I wanted across multiple books. So I moved the content doc to a spreadsheet which visually represents the content reqirements that helped me solve the problems.

Spreedsheet I created to visualizing the real content spec

Insights

Turns out that the two post-reading question types have very distinct content structures. The design need to be dynamic to the type.

Question type A

  • Straightforward
  • Single choice for each bucket
  • always 4 answer choices
  • Answer choices could have long text

    • Question type B

  • more tricky
  • single or multiple choice question
  • any number between 4-10 answer choices
  • answer choices are really short
    • Identify problems
    • This layout for K-2 doesn’t fit the more complex content reqirements for grades 3-5
    • Students had trouble with figuring out the draging interaction
    • Students can’t see the labels for the two target zones on the top once it’s filled with answer.

    Interactive behavior of a post-reading question in grade K-2 book

    The solution

    Knowing that 10 answer choices might be too much for kids to manage, and hard to fit them all on the screen while keeping everything in a readable size, I discused with the content team, and they agreed to change it down to 6 choices.

    I explored the idea of having slightly different layouts for each type to fit the two very different sets.

    Conclusion

    Measuring success

    Questions to measure success:

    • Given a limited timeframe, would student demonstrate a higher level of understanding when reading on the single column view compare to the old layout?
    • Are students able to find a given text in the book faster in the single column view than in the two column view?
    • Are students able to work on the questions on their own and understand whether their submision was correct or incorrect?

    To be able to answer these questions, we would require qualitive testing, and it’s hard to get answer from only looking at the quantitive data report. Because we were short on time to fit it into release schedule, some design was implemented without addintional playtesting to validate.

    But a couple month after releasing, we were able to see a 13% decrease of unanswered question on post-reading question types, and 10% decrease on all question types compare to K-2. Although there are other factors going into the numbers, such as content difficulty, or students developed their technigical skills, I was still happy with the results.

    Design lessons learned

    • Layout and typography are integral in digital reading experience.

    • Design with real content whenever possible to reduce the greater cost of changing the design at later stage of development cycle.

    • What’s obvious to designer may not be to user, subtle cues don’t always work.
